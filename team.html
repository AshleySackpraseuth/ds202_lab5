The more dangerous season depends on the state, as seen by the chloropeth math. Dangerous was measured by the number of fatal accidents in a state. In order to acheive showing both the season and number of fatal accidents in one map, I multiplied the number of fatal accidents in states that had winter as their more dangerous season by -1. So although the negative scale on the right might not make the most sense at first (because a negative amount of deaths is not possible), visually, the map is still intuitive. The darker the state, the more fatal accidents occurred in the winter in that state. The lighter the state, the more fatal accidents occurred in the summer in that state. Majority of states had more fatal accidents in the summer than winter (which is confirmed by the table before the map). This may be because people have more leisure time to go out and make reckless decisions in the summer. I was surprised to see Florida have the most fatal accidents in the winter because I didnt think the season-change affected Florida; I thought it was always hot all-year round. California and Texas have the highest number of fatal accidents occur in the summer but this may also be due to the fact that these states have a larger population compared to other states. Fatal accidents/per capita would give us a more accurate view of how the seasons affect the number of fatal traffic accidents.
